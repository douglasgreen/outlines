# Problem Solving

## Introduction

Problem solving is an essential skill, one that we all encounter in various forms every day. Its
importance cannot be overstated, both in personal and professional contexts. In this introduction,
we will explore what problem solving is, its significance in different areas of life, and provide an
overview of the subject.

### Definition of Problem Solving

At its core, problem solving is the process of identifying a problem, understanding its root causes,
and devising effective strategies to overcome it. It involves a series of steps or stages, ranging
from the initial recognition of a problem to the implementation and evaluation of solutions. This
process requires not only logical thinking but also creativity, as it often involves navigating
unforeseen challenges and complex scenarios.

Problem solving is not just about finding quick fixes. It's about understanding the deeper issues at
hand and developing solutions that are sustainable and effective in the long run. This skill
transcends simply addressing immediate issues; it’s about building a framework that prevents future
problems or at least makes them more manageable.

### Importance in Personal and Professional Life

In personal life, problem-solving skills help individuals navigate everyday challenges — from
managing finances and maintaining relationships to making significant life decisions. It equips
people with the ability to assess situations critically, weigh options, and make informed decisions.
These skills are crucial in developing resilience, enabling individuals to adapt and thrive in the
face of life's uncertainties.

In the professional realm, problem-solving skills are invaluable. They are at the heart of
innovation and efficiency in the workplace. In a rapidly evolving business landscape, the ability to
identify problems quickly and solve them efficiently can give a competitive edge to an organization.
It fosters a culture of continuous improvement, where challenges are seen as opportunities for
growth and learning.

### Overview of the Subject

Problem solving is a multifaceted subject that incorporates various techniques, methodologies, and
mindsets. It involves understanding different types of problems — from simple, linear issues to
complex, systemic challenges. The subject also delves into the psychological aspects of problem
solving, such as overcoming cognitive biases and fostering a growth mindset.

Furthermore, problem solving in today's world often requires a collaborative approach. It involves
working within teams, leveraging diverse perspectives, and utilizing collective knowledge and
skills. The role of technology and digital tools in facilitating problem solving, especially in
complex scenarios, is also a critical aspect of this subject.

In subsequent chapters, we will explore the various dimensions of problem solving in more depth.
From the basic steps involved in the problem-solving process to advanced techniques and tools, this
guide aims to equip readers with the skills and knowledge to tackle problems effectively, both in
their personal and professional lives. We will also look at real-world examples and case studies to
illustrate how effective problem solving can lead to significant improvements and innovations in
various spheres of life.

## Understanding Problems

Problems are inherent in every aspect of our lives and understanding them is the first step towards
finding effective solutions. In this section, we'll delve into the various types of problems, their
characteristics, and how to recognize them.

### Types of Problems

Problems can be categorized in several ways, based on their nature, scope, and the approach needed
to solve them. These categories help us in understanding and strategizing the problem-solving
process.

#### Simple vs. Complex

-   **Simple Problems:** These are straightforward issues with clear solutions. They usually have a
    limited number of variables and the outcomes are predictable. An example of a simple problem
    could be fixing a broken appliance.

-   **Complex Problems:** These are multifaceted and involve multiple variables and stakeholders.
    They are characterized by uncertainty and the interdependence of various factors. Complex
    problems require a systemic approach to solve, like addressing climate change or organizational
    restructuring.

#### Predictable vs. Unpredictable

-   **Predictable Problems:** These problems occur regularly and their outcomes are known based on
    past experiences. They are often procedural and can be solved through established methods or
    protocols.

-   **Unpredictable Problems:** These arise suddenly and without clear precedent, making their
    outcomes uncertain. Solving unpredictable problems often requires innovative thinking and
    adaptability, such as dealing with a sudden market crash or an unexpected health crisis.

#### Personal vs. Professional

-   **Personal Problems:** These are challenges faced in one's personal life, such as relationship
    issues, health concerns, or personal financial planning.

-   **Professional Problems:** These occur in the context of one's work or business environment.
    They can range from project management issues, team dynamics, to strategic business challenges.

### Common Characteristics of Problems

Despite their diversity, most problems share certain common characteristics:

-   **A Gap:** A problem represents a gap between the current state and the desired state.
-   **Need for Solution:** There is a need or demand for a solution.
-   **Obstacles:** There are barriers or challenges that need to be overcome.
-   **Resources and Constraints:** Problems often have to be solved within certain limitations or
    constraints, like time, budget, or resources.

### Recognizing a Problem

Recognizing a problem is not always as straightforward as it seems. It involves an awareness of
something being amiss and the ability to articulate what exactly the issue is. Here are some steps
to recognize a problem effectively:

1. **Observation:** Paying attention to the signs or symptoms that indicate something is not working
   as intended.
2. **Comparison:** Comparing the current situation with the expected or desired state.
3. **Questioning:** Asking critical questions about why the gap exists and what might be causing it.
4. **Feedback:** Seeking input from others, as different perspectives can help in identifying
   problems that might not be immediately apparent.

Understanding problems in their various forms and nuances is crucial in developing effective
solutions. By categorizing and recognizing problems accurately, we can employ the most appropriate
problem-solving techniques and strategies, thereby enhancing our ability to navigate challenges in
both our personal and professional lives.

## Problem-Solving Methods

Problem-solving methods are diverse, each offering unique perspectives and strategies to tackle
challenges. Understanding these methods can greatly enhance our ability to find effective solutions.
Let’s explore some of the primary approaches: the Analytical Approach, Creative Approach, Systems
Thinking, and Comparative Analysis, along with their pros and cons.

### Analytical Approach

The analytical approach involves breaking down a problem into smaller components and examining them
in a detailed, systematic way. It often includes data collection, statistical analysis, and the use
of logical reasoning to understand the problem and identify solutions.

**Pros:**

-   Highly logical and structured, leading to clear, data-driven solutions.
-   Effective for problems where quantitative analysis is essential.
-   Reduces complexity by breaking down problems into manageable parts.

**Cons:**

-   Can be time-consuming and may require specialized knowledge or tools.
-   May overlook qualitative aspects like human emotions or cultural factors.
-   Can lead to rigid thinking, limiting creative solutions.

### Creative Approach

The creative approach emphasizes thinking outside the box and generating innovative, often
unconventional solutions. It includes brainstorming, lateral thinking, and the exploration of
various possibilities without immediate judgment.

**Pros:**

-   Encourages innovative and novel solutions.
-   Flexible and adaptable to different types of problems.
-   Fosters a more engaging and enjoyable problem-solving process.

**Cons:**

-   May lead to impractical or unfeasible solutions.
-   Harder to quantify and evaluate ideas.
-   Requires a certain level of creativity and open-mindedness that may not be innate to all
    individuals or teams.

### Systems Thinking

Systems thinking involves understanding a problem as part of a larger system, considering the
interrelationships and interactions between different components. It looks at the broader picture
and focuses on patterns, trends, and the dynamic nature of systems.

**Pros:**

-   Offers a holistic view of complex problems.
-   Helps in identifying long-term solutions and potential unintended consequences.
-   Encourages consideration of multiple stakeholders and variables.

**Cons:**

-   Can be overwhelming due to its complexity and the vast range of factors involved.
-   Requires a deep understanding of the system, which can be time-consuming to acquire.
-   May not be suitable for simple, straightforward problems.

### Comparative Analysis

Comparative analysis involves comparing different problems or solutions to identify the best
approach. It often includes benchmarking against best practices or learning from similar case
studies.

**Pros:**

-   Provides a practical perspective based on existing solutions or problems.
-   Can speed up the problem-solving process by leveraging known solutions.
-   Helps in understanding the effectiveness of different strategies.

**Cons:**

-   Risks adopting a one-size-fits-all solution that may not be entirely suitable for the specific
    problem.
-   May discourage innovative thinking if too much focus is placed on existing solutions.
-   Relies on the availability of comparable situations, which may not always be present.

### Conclusion

Each problem-solving method has its strengths and weaknesses, and often the best approach is to use
a combination of these methods. Analytical and systems thinking provide structure and a
comprehensive view, while creative and comparative approaches bring innovation and practical
insights. The choice of method depends on the nature of the problem, the context, and the desired
outcomes. By understanding and applying these methods appropriately, problem solvers can
significantly enhance their ability to address challenges effectively and efficiently.

## Steps in Problem Solving

Effective problem solving typically follows a structured process, involving several key steps. These
steps help in systematically tackling the problem, from initial identification to the implementation
of solutions. Let’s discuss each of these steps in detail.

### 1. Identifying the Problem

The first step is to clearly define and understand the problem. This involves recognizing that a
problem exists and then articulating what it is.

-   **Understanding the Context:** Consider the circumstances or environment where the problem
    exists.
-   **Stating the Problem:** Define the problem in a clear and concise manner.
-   **Differentiating Symptoms from the Problem:** Ensure that what is being addressed is the actual
    problem, not just its symptoms.

### 2. Gathering Information

Once the problem is identified, the next step is to collect relevant information.

-   **Data Collection:** Gather data that is pertinent to the problem. This could include
    quantitative data, qualitative observations, or background information.
-   **Consulting with Stakeholders:** Engage with individuals or groups affected by the problem to
    gain different perspectives.
-   **Research:** Look into similar problems or case studies for insights.

### 3. Generating Possible Solutions

With a clear understanding of the problem and the necessary information in hand, the next step is
brainstorming potential solutions.

-   **Creative Ideation:** Encourage free-thinking and creativity to come up with a range of
    solutions.
-   **Inclusivity in Ideas:** Consider ideas from all sources, without immediate judgment.
-   **Diversity of Solutions:** Aim for a diverse set of solutions, covering various approaches and
    perspectives.

### 4. Evaluating and Selecting Solutions

After generating a list of possible solutions, evaluate them to determine the most feasible and
effective option.

-   **Criteria for Evaluation:** Establish criteria for evaluating solutions, such as cost,
    feasibility, time, resources, and impact.
-   **Pros and Cons:** Analyze the advantages and disadvantages of each solution.
-   **Decision-Making:** Choose the solution that best fits the problem and aligns with the goals
    and constraints.

### 5. Implementing the Solution

The chosen solution is then put into action.

-   **Action Plan:** Develop a detailed plan for implementation, including steps, timeline, and
    responsibilities.
-   **Resource Allocation:** Ensure that the necessary resources (time, money, personnel) are
    available.
-   **Monitoring:** Keep track of the implementation process and make adjustments as needed.

### 6. Review and Learn from the Process

After the solution is implemented, it’s important to review the process and the outcome.

-   **Assessment:** Evaluate the effectiveness of the solution in solving the problem.
-   **Feedback:** Gather feedback from stakeholders.
-   **Learning:** Identify lessons learned and how the process could be improved for future
    problem-solving efforts.

### Conclusion

This structured approach to problem solving ensures a comprehensive and systematic handling of
problems. By meticulously working through these steps, individuals and organizations can effectively
tackle challenges, arrive at efficient solutions, and continuously improve their problem-solving
capabilities. Each step plays a crucial role in ensuring that the solution is not only effective but
also sustainable and adaptable to future changes or challenges.

## Tools and Techniques

Problem-solving can be significantly enhanced by using various tools and techniques. These methods
help structure the process, generate insights, and lead to more effective solutions. Let's explore
some key tools and techniques: Brainstorming, Root Cause Analysis, Mind Mapping, SWOT Analysis, and
the Decision Matrix.

### 1. Brainstorming

Brainstorming is a technique used to generate a large number of ideas in a short period. It's often
used in the early stages of problem-solving to explore possible solutions.

-   **How It Works:** Typically involves a group session where participants are encouraged to share
    their ideas freely without criticism.
-   **Benefits:** Encourages creativity and can yield a diverse range of solutions.
-   **Tips for Effective Brainstorming:** Set a clear objective, encourage participation from all
    group members, withhold criticism during the session, and record all ideas for later analysis.

### 2. Root Cause Analysis

Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is a method used to identify the underlying causes of a problem.

-   **Techniques within RCA:** The "Five Whys" technique (asking "why" repeatedly to peel back the
    layers of a problem) and the Fishbone Diagram (which visually maps out the causes of a problem).
-   **Benefits:** Helps in understanding the fundamental reasons for a problem, preventing
    recurrence.
-   **Application:** Particularly useful in quality control and process improvement.

### 3. Mind Mapping

Mind Mapping is a visual tool that helps in organizing information, generating ideas, and making
connections.

-   **How It Works:** Begins with a central idea and branches out into related topics and subtopics,
    often using keywords and images.
-   **Benefits:** Enhances creative thinking and organization of thoughts. It can be a powerful tool
    for brainstorming, planning, and problem-solving.
-   **Usage:** Can be used individually or in group settings, with physical charts or digital tools.

### 4. SWOT Analysis

SWOT Analysis is a strategic planning tool used to identify Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities,
and Threats related to a problem or situation.

-   **Components:**
    -   Strengths and Weaknesses: Internal factors.
    -   Opportunities and Threats: External factors.
-   **Benefits:** Provides a comprehensive overview of the internal and external factors affecting a
    problem or decision.
-   **Application:** Widely used in business strategy, project management, and personal development
    planning.

### 5. Decision Matrix

A Decision Matrix (or Prioritization Matrix) is a tool used to compare and prioritize a list of
options based on specific, weighted criteria.

-   **How It Works:** Involves listing options and criteria, assigning weights to each criterion,
    and then rating each option against these criteria.
-   **Benefits:** Helps in making objective decisions when multiple criteria are involved and
    simplifies complex decision-making processes.
-   **Usage:** Useful in situations where decisions need to be made based on a balanced assessment
    of multiple factors, such as resource allocation or product feature prioritization.

### Conclusion

These tools and techniques offer structured ways to approach problem-solving, each with its unique
strengths. Brainstorming and Mind Mapping are excellent for generating ideas and organizing
thoughts. Root Cause Analysis is key in understanding the deeper issues behind problems. SWOT
Analysis provides a broad perspective on a problem, considering internal and external factors.
Finally, the Decision Matrix is invaluable for making complex decisions with multiple variables.
Using these tools in combination can significantly enhance the problem-solving process, leading to
more effective and sustainable solutions.

## Group Problem Solving

Group problem solving involves a team working together to address a challenge or issue. This
collaborative approach can offer unique benefits but also comes with its own set of challenges.
Understanding these dynamics and employing effective techniques can significantly enhance the
outcome.

### Advantages and Challenges

**Advantages:**

1. **Diverse Perspectives:** A group brings together varied experiences and viewpoints, which can
   lead to more comprehensive and creative solutions.
2. **Synergy:** The collective efforts and ideas can create a synergy where the group's total output
   is greater than the sum of individual contributions.
3. **Shared Responsibility:** The burden of solving complex issues is distributed, reducing pressure
   on individuals.

**Challenges:**

1. **Groupthink:** A tendency to conform to the majority view can stifle creativity and lead to
   suboptimal solutions.
2. **Conflict:** Differences in opinions and personalities can lead to conflicts, potentially
   hampering the problem-solving process.
3. **Time-Consuming:** Reaching consensus in a group can be time-consuming compared to individual
   decision-making.

### Group Dynamics

Understanding and managing group dynamics is crucial for effective problem solving:

1. **Roles and Responsibilities:** Clearly defined roles can help manage expectations and streamline
   the process.
2. **Communication:** Open and respectful communication is essential for sharing ideas and
   addressing disagreements.
3. **Leadership:** Effective leadership can guide the group, maintain focus, and facilitate a
   democratic decision-making process.

### Techniques for Effective Group Problem Solving

1. **Brainstorming Sessions:** Encourage free and open sharing of ideas without immediate criticism.
2. **Nominal Group Technique:** This involves individuals generating ideas independently and then
   bringing them together for discussion and ranking, which can reduce the influence of dominant
   personalities.
3. **Root Cause Analysis:** As a group, identify the root causes of the problem rather than focusing
   on symptoms.
4. **Conflict Resolution Strategies:** Employ techniques such as active listening and mediation to
   manage and resolve conflicts.
5. **Regular Check-Ins:** Schedule meetings to assess progress, address challenges, and adjust
   strategies as needed.

### Case Studies of Successful Group Solutions

1. **NASA’s Apollo 13 Mission:** The successful resolution of the Apollo 13 crisis is a classic
   example of group problem solving under pressure. The team's ability to work collaboratively,
   under extreme stress and with limited resources, led to the safe return of the astronauts.

2. **Corporate Turnaround - IBM in the 1990s:** IBM’s turnaround from near bankruptcy was a result
   of a strategic shift implemented through group problem-solving. The leadership, along with teams
   across various departments, collaboratively restructured the company’s operations and strategy,
   leading to a remarkable recovery.

3. **Community-Led Solutions in Public Health:** There are numerous instances where community
   involvement in public health issues has led to innovative solutions. For example, community
   health workers in certain regions have collaboratively addressed local health challenges through
   tailored education and intervention programs.

### Conclusion

Group problem solving, when managed effectively, can yield highly successful outcomes. The key is to
leverage the diverse strengths of the group, ensure effective communication and leadership, and
employ structured problem-solving techniques. Understanding and navigating the challenges of group
dynamics are crucial in harnessing the collective intelligence of the team.

## Communication in Problem Solving

Effective communication plays a pivotal role in problem solving. It ensures that ideas, data, and
solutions are clearly understood and appropriately actioned. This involves not just talking but also
listening, persuading, negotiating, and presenting solutions effectively. Let's explore these
aspects in more detail.

### Role of Effective Communication

Communication in problem solving serves several key functions:

1. **Clarity:** It helps in clearly defining the problem and in articulating the proposed solutions.
2. **Collaboration:** Facilitates collaboration, ensuring that all team members are on the same page
   and contributing effectively.
3. **Conflict Resolution:** Effective communication is critical in resolving misunderstandings and
   conflicts that can arise during the problem-solving process.
4. **Information Sharing:** It allows for the efficient exchange of information, data, and feedback,
   which are essential for finding and implementing solutions.

### Listening Skills

Listening is as important as speaking in problem solving:

1. **Active Listening:** This involves fully concentrating, understanding, responding, and then
   remembering what is being said. It's about engaging with the speaker beyond just hearing their
   words.
2. **Empathetic Listening:** Understanding and acknowledging the emotions behind what is being said.
   This builds trust and can lead to a more open exchange of ideas.
3. **Feedback:** Providing thoughtful feedback shows that you are engaged and understand the
   discussion, which can encourage more open communication.

### Persuasion and Negotiation

In many problem-solving scenarios, you may need to persuade others to accept a solution or negotiate
to reach a consensus:

1. **Persuasion:** This involves convincing others about the viability and benefits of a particular
   solution. It requires clear articulation, backed by facts and logical reasoning.
2. **Negotiation:** This is the process of reaching a mutually acceptable agreement. It requires
   understanding the needs and constraints of all parties involved and finding a solution that
   accommodates these as much as possible.

### Presenting Solutions

The way solutions are communicated can significantly impact their acceptance and implementation:

1. **Clarity and Conciseness:** Present solutions in a clear, concise, and structured manner. Avoid
   jargon and overly complex explanations.
2. **Tailoring the Message:** Adapt your communication style to your audience. Technical
   stakeholders might appreciate data and facts, while others may need more context and simpler
   explanations.
3. **Visual Aids:** Utilizing charts, graphs, and diagrams can help in making complex information
   more understandable.
4. **Feedback Mechanisms:** Allow for questions and feedback to ensure that the solution is
   understood and any concerns are addressed.

### Conclusion

In conclusion, effective communication is a cornerstone of successful problem solving. It
encompasses clear expression, attentive listening, skillful persuasion and negotiation, and the
effective presentation of solutions. Each aspect plays a critical role in ensuring that problems are
accurately understood, solutions are collaboratively developed, and the implementation is
effectively executed. By honing these communication skills, individuals and teams can significantly
enhance their problem-solving capabilities.

## Real-World Problem-Solving Examples

Problem-solving is a critical skill across various domains, from business to social issues and
personal challenges. Examining real-world examples helps illustrate how effective problem-solving
strategies can be applied in diverse contexts. Let's explore some of these scenarios:

### Business Case Studies

1. **Toyota’s Production System:**

    - **Problem:** Toyota faced significant challenges in managing production efficiency and quality
      control.
    - **Solution:** They implemented the Toyota Production System (TPS), which introduced 'Lean
      Manufacturing' and 'Just-In-Time' production.
    - **Outcome:** This led to reduced waste, improved quality, and increased efficiency,
      revolutionizing manufacturing processes worldwide.

2. **Netflix’s Transition to Streaming:**
    - **Problem:** With the rise of digital streaming, Netflix’s DVD rental model was becoming
      obsolete.
    - **Solution:** Netflix shifted its business model to online streaming, investing heavily in
      technology and content creation.
    - **Outcome:** This pivot made Netflix a global leader in the streaming industry, dramatically
      changing media consumption patterns.

### Social Issues

1. **Plastic Waste Management:**

    - **Problem:** The accumulation of plastic waste has become a significant environmental
      challenge.
    - **Solution:** Many countries and organizations have implemented initiatives like banning
      single-use plastics, promoting recycling, and developing biodegradable plastic alternatives.
    - **Outcome:** These efforts have led to reduced plastic waste and increased public awareness of
      environmental sustainability.

2. **Eradication of Smallpox:**
    - **Problem:** Smallpox was a deadly disease affecting millions worldwide.
    - **Solution:** A global vaccination campaign was launched by the World Health Organization.
    - **Outcome:** By 1980, smallpox was declared eradicated, showcasing the power of coordinated
      global public health initiatives.

### Personal Challenges

1. **Career Transition:**

    - **Problem:** An individual may face the challenge of a career transition, perhaps due to
      market changes or personal dissatisfaction.
    - **Solution:** This might involve identifying transferable skills, seeking additional education
      or training, and networking in the new field.
    - **Outcome:** Successfully transitioning into a new career path that aligns better with one's
      skills and interests.

2. **Debt Management:**
    - **Problem:** Managing personal debt can be a significant challenge.
    - **Solution:** Strategies might include creating a budget, consolidating debts, and seeking
      financial advice.
    - **Outcome:** Effective management of debts, avoiding financial crises, and achieving financial
      stability.

### Conclusion

These examples demonstrate how problem-solving skills are applicable and vital across various
realms. In business, it's about innovation and adaptability; in social issues, the focus is on
collaborative and sustainable solutions; and in personal challenges, it involves strategic planning
and self-reflection. Each scenario requires a unique approach, but the core principles of effective
problem solving remain constant: understanding the issue, exploring solutions, implementing
strategies, and learning from outcomes.

## Glossary of Terms

1. **Problem Solving:** The process of identifying a challenge or obstacle and finding effective
   solutions to overcome it.

2. **Analytical Thinking:** A method of problem solving that involves breaking down complex problems
   into smaller parts and examining them systematically.

3. **Creative Thinking:** The ability to think outside the box and generate innovative and novel
   solutions to problems.

4. **Brainstorming:** A group creativity technique used to generate a large number of ideas for the
   solution to a problem.

5. **Root Cause Analysis:** A method of problem solving that focuses on identifying the root causes
   of problems or events rather than simply addressing their symptoms.

6. **Systems Thinking:** An approach to problem solving that views problems as parts of an overall
   system, rather than in isolation, emphasizing the interconnections and interactions between
   elements.

7. **Decision Matrix:** A tool used to compare and prioritize options by evaluating them against
   predetermined criteria.

8. **SWOT Analysis:** A strategic planning tool used to identify Strengths, Weaknesses,
   Opportunities, and Threats related to a business or project.

9. **Mind Mapping:** A visual tool used to represent ideas and concepts around a central problem,
   making connections and exploring relationships.

10. **Five Whys:** A problem-solving technique that involves repeatedly asking the question "Why?"
    to drill down into the root cause of a problem.

11. **Heuristic:** A problem-solving approach that employs a practical method or various shortcuts
    to produce solutions that may not be perfect but are sufficient for the immediate goals.

12. **Algorithm:** A step-by-step procedure or formula for solving a problem, often used in the
    field of mathematics and computer science.

13. **Critical Thinking:** The ability to think clearly and rationally, understanding the logical
    connection between ideas, and critically assessing solutions.

14. **Fishbone Diagram:** A visual tool used in root cause analysis to identify, explore, and
    display the possible causes of a particular problem.

15. **Benchmarking:** The process of comparing one's business processes and performance metrics to
    industry bests or best practices from other companies.

16. **Conflict Resolution:** The process of resolving a dispute or a conflict by providing each
    side's needs and adequately addressing their interests.

17. **Feedback Loop:** A process in which the outputs of a system are circled back and used as
    inputs, facilitating continuous improvement and adaptation.

18. **Risk Assessment:** The identification, evaluation, and estimation of the levels of risks
    involved in a situation, with respect to external and internal factors.

19. **Innovation:** The process of translating an idea or invention into a good or service that
    creates value or for which customers will pay.

20. **Project Management:** The practice of initiating, planning, executing, controlling, and
    closing the work of a team to achieve specific goals and meet specific success criteria.
