# Algebra

## The Essence of Algebra

### Introduction to Algebra
Algebra is a branch of mathematics that deals with symbols and the rules for manipulating these symbols. It is a language of its own that allows us to translate real-world problems into mathematical forms and then solve them. At its core, algebra involves working with unknown values (variables), constants, and the operations that relate them. Unlike arithmetic, which deals primarily with specific numerical values, algebra introduces the concept of variables, which can represent any number. This shift from concrete numbers to abstract symbols is what gives algebra its power and flexibility.

### Historical Background
Algebra has a rich history that dates back to ancient civilizations. Its roots can be traced to the Babylonians, who developed advanced arithmetical systems as early as 1900 BC. However, the term "algebra" itself and much of its modern foundation are attributed to the Persian mathematician Al-Khwarizmi in the 9th century. His work, "The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing," introduced systematic ways of solving linear and quadratic equations. This laid the groundwork for modern algebraic notation and problem-solving methods. Over the centuries, algebra evolved with contributions from mathematicians across various cultures, including Greek, Indian, and Arab scholars.

### Importance and Applications in Various Fields
The significance of algebra lies in its universality and its power to solve a wide range of problems. Here are some key areas where algebra is crucial:

1. **Science and Engineering**: Algebra is foundational in physics, chemistry, biology, and engineering. It helps in modeling natural phenomena, from the movement of planets to the principles of genetics. In engineering, algebraic equations are used to design systems and solve practical problems like calculating forces, optimizing design parameters, and analyzing electrical circuits.

2. **Economics and Finance**: Algebraic models are essential in economics for analyzing trends, making forecasts, and optimizing resources. In finance, algebra is used in calculating interest rates, risk assessments, and in the valuation of financial instruments like bonds and stocks.

3. **Computer Science**: Algorithms, the core of computer programming and data processing, often rely on algebraic expressions. Algebra is also critical in cryptography, computer graphics, and database design.

4. **Statistics and Data Analysis**: Algebra is fundamental in statistics for the formulation of data models, interpreting survey data, and performing various types of data analysis which are crucial in decision-making processes in various sectors.

5. **Everyday Problem Solving**: Algebraic thinking enables us to solve everyday problems, such as figuring out the quantity of materials needed for a project, or managing budgets.

In conclusion, algebra is more than just a subject within mathematics; it is a vital tool that provides a framework for rational thinking and problem-solving across various disciplines. Its historical evolution reflects the human quest for understanding and manipulating abstract concepts, and its applications in modern society are vast and continually expanding.

## Basic Mathematical Operations

### Basic Mathematical Operations

### Review of Arithmetic Operations
Arithmetic operations are the foundation of both basic mathematics and algebra. They include:

1. **Addition (+)**: The simplest arithmetic operation, addition combines two or more numbers into a single sum. For example, $`3 + 2 = 5`$.

2. **Subtraction (−)**: This operation represents the difference between numbers. For instance, $`5 - 2 = 3`$.

3. **Multiplication (× or $`\cdot`$)**: Multiplication is the process of adding a number to itself a certain number of times. For example, $`4 \times 3`$ means adding 4 three times, which equals 12.

4. **Division (÷ or /)**: This operation involves splitting a number into equal parts. For instance, $`12 ÷ 3 = 4`$.

These operations are not just limited to numbers; they are also applied to algebraic expressions.

### Introduction to Algebraic Expressions
An algebraic expression is a mathematical phrase that can contain ordinary numbers, variables (like x or y), and operations (such as add, subtract, multiply, and divide). Unlike an equation, an algebraic expression doesn't have an equality sign (=). Examples include $`2x + 3`$, $`x^2 - 4y`$, or $`3abc`$.

1. **Variables**: These are symbols that represent unknown values. In the expression $`2x + 3`$, $`x`$ is a variable.

2. **Constants**: These are fixed numbers. In $`2x + 3`$, 3 is a constant.

3. **Coefficients**: These are numbers used to multiply a variable. In $`2x`$, 2 is the coefficient.

### Simplifying Expressions
Simplifying an expression means rewriting it in the most concise way without changing its value. This can involve:

1. **Combining like terms**: Terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. For example, in $`2x + 3x`$, $`2x`$ and $`3x`$ are like terms and can be added to get $`5x`$.

2. **Using the distributive property**: This is often used when you have a multiplication over addition or subtraction, like in the expression $`3(x + 4)`$. Distributing the 3 gives $`3x + 12`$.

3. **Removing parentheses**: This is often done in conjunction with the distributive property. For example, simplifying $`2(3x + 5)`$ involves distributing the 2, resulting in $`6x + 10`$.

4. **Reducing fractions**: If an algebraic expression contains fractions, you can simplify it by reducing the fractions to their lowest terms.

In summary, understanding and being able to manipulate basic mathematical operations is essential for working with algebraic expressions. The transition from arithmetic to algebra is marked by the introduction of variables and expressions, which represent generalized forms of arithmetic operations. Simplifying expressions is a key skill in algebra that involves combining like terms, using the distributive property, and reducing fractions to make expressions easier to work with.

## Understanding Variables and Constants

### Definition and Use of Variables and Constants

1. **Variables**: A variable in algebra is a symbol, often a letter like $`x`$, $`y`$, or $`z`$, that represents an unknown or changeable value. In equations and expressions, variables are placeholders for numbers that can vary or are yet to be determined. For example, in the equation $`x + 5 = 10`$, $`x`$ is a variable.

2. **Constants**: Constants are fixed values that do not change. In algebra, constants are typically represented by numbers. For instance, in the expression $`3x + 4`$, the number 4 is a constant. It remains the same regardless of the value of $`x`$.

Variables are used to generalize mathematical relationships and principles, making them applicable to a wide range of situations. Constants, on the other hand, provide specific, unchanging values that anchor these relationships.

### Basic Operations with Variables

Variables can undergo the same basic operations as numbers: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. However, when performing these operations with variables, the specific value of the variable is not always known. Instead, the operations focus on manipulating the variables symbolically. For example:

- **Addition/Subtraction**: Variables can be added or subtracted if they are "like terms" (i.e., they have the same variable part). For instance, $`3x + 2x = 5x`$ but $`3x + 2y`$ cannot be simplified further.
  
- **Multiplication**: When multiplying variables, their coefficients (numerical parts) are multiplied together, and their variable parts are combined following the rules of exponents. For instance, $`3x \times 2x = 6x^2`$.

- **Division**: Variables can be divided, and this often involves simplifying the expression by canceling out common terms in the numerator and denominator. For instance, $`\frac{6x^2}{3x} = 2x`$.

### Concept of Like and Unlike Terms

In algebra, terms are considered "like terms" if they have the same variable raised to the same power. For example, $`2x`$ and $`5x`$ are like terms because they both contain the variable $`x`$ raised to the first power. This similarity allows them to be combined through addition or subtraction.

On the other hand, "unlike terms" have different variables or the same variables raised to different powers. For example, $`2x`$ and $`2y`$ are unlike terms, as are $`x^2`$ and $`x^3`$. Unlike terms cannot be combined through addition or subtraction.

Understanding the distinction between like and unlike terms is crucial in algebra, particularly when simplifying expressions. It ensures that only terms with the same variable components are combined, maintaining the integrity of the mathematical relationships represented by the expressions.

## Solving Simple Equations

### Linear Equations in One Variable
Linear equations in one variable are basic algebraic equations where the highest power of the variable is one. They generally take the form $`ax + b = c`$, where $`a`$, $`b`$, and $`c`$ are constants, and $`x`$ is the variable. The goal is to find the value of $`x`$ that makes the equation true.

### Techniques for Solving Equations
1. **Isolating the Variable**: The primary technique in solving linear equations is to isolate the variable on one side of the equation. This is done through basic operations - addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

2. **Inverse Operations**: Use inverse operations to cancel out terms and simplify the equation. If the variable is added to a number, subtract the same number from both sides to eliminate it, and vice versa. Similarly, use division to cancel multiplication and vice versa.

3. **Balancing the Equation**: Always perform the same operation on both sides of the equation to maintain balance. This is based on the principle that if two expressions are equal, they remain equal if the same operation is applied to both.

4. **Checking the Solution**: Once a solution is found, it's important to check it by substituting it back into the original equation to verify if it satisfies the equation.

### Practical Examples and Applications
1. **Example Problem**: Solve $`3x + 4 = 19`$.
   - **Step 1**: Subtract 4 from both sides: $`3x + 4 - 4 = 19 - 4`$, which simplifies to $`3x = 15`$.
   - **Step 2**: Divide both sides by 3: $`\frac{3x}{3} = \frac{15}{3}`$, simplifying to $`x = 5`$.
   - **Step 3**: Check by substituting $`x`$ back into the original equation: $`3(5) + 4 = 19`$, which holds true.

2. **Practical Application - Budgeting**: Suppose you have a budget of $300 for a party and you have already spent $80. If each party item costs $10, how many items can you buy? Let $`x`$ be the number of items. The equation would be $`10x + 80 = 300`$. Solving this, you find the number of items you can purchase.

3. **Practical Application - Distance Problems**: If you know the speed at which you are traveling and the time you want to travel, you can find the distance covered. For example, if you travel at 60 mph for 3 hours, the distance covered is given by $`d = rt`$, where $`d`$ is distance, $`r`$ is rate, and $`t`$ is time. Here, it becomes $`d = 60 \times 3`$, which equals 180 miles.

In conclusion, solving linear equations is a fundamental skill in algebra, with diverse applications in various real-life scenarios, from financial planning to solving distance problems. The techniques of isolating the variable, using inverse operations, balancing the equation, and checking the solution are crucial steps in this process.

## The Power of Exponents

### Introduction to Exponents
Exponents are a way to represent repeated multiplication of the same number. In the expression $`a^n`$, $`a`$ is the base and $`n`$ is the exponent, indicating that $`a`$ is multiplied by itself $`n`$ times. For example, $`3^4`$ (read as "three to the fourth power") means $`3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3`$, which equals 81.

### Rules of Exponents
Several fundamental rules govern the operations involving exponents:

1. **Product Rule**: When multiplying two powers with the same base, add their exponents. For example, $`a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}`$.

2. **Quotient Rule**: When dividing two powers with the same base, subtract the exponent of the denominator from the exponent of the numerator. For example, $`\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}`$, where $`n \neq 0`$.

3. **Power of a Power Rule**: When raising an exponential expression to another power, multiply the exponents. For example, $`(a^m)^n = a^{mn}`$.

4. **Zero Exponent Rule**: Any nonzero number raised to the power of zero is 1. For example, $`a^0 = 1`$, provided that $`a \neq 0`$.

5. **Negative Exponent Rule**: A negative exponent indicates the reciprocal of the base raised to the absolute value of the exponent. For example, $`a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}`$, where $`a \neq 0`$.

6. **Product to a Power**: When a product of bases is raised to an exponent, the exponent applies to each base. For example, $`(ab)^n = a^n \times b^n`$.

7. **Fraction to a Power**: When a fraction is raised to an exponent, both the numerator and the denominator are raised to that exponent. For example, $`\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}`$, where $`b \neq 0`$.

### Exponential Expressions and Equations
Exponential expressions involve variables in the exponent. For example, $`2^x`$ is an exponential expression where $`x`$ is the exponent.

Solving exponential equations often requires finding the value of the variable in the exponent. These equations can be more complex than linear or quadratic equations and may require different techniques, such as:

1. **Logarithms**: If an equation is in the form $`a^x = b`$, taking the logarithm of both sides can help solve for $`x`$. For example, using the natural logarithm, $`\ln(a^x) = \ln(b)`$ implies $`x = \frac{\ln(b)}{\ln(a)}`$.

2. **Setting Bases Equal**: If an equation involves two exponential expressions with the same base, their exponents can be set equal to each other. For example, in the equation $`3^{2x} = 3^5`$, it follows that $`2x = 5`$.

3. **Graphical Methods**: Graphical solutions involve plotting the exponential equation and identifying the points where it intersects with the x-axis or other functions.

Exponents play a vital role in various fields, including compound interest calculations in finance, exponential growth models in biology, and scientific notation in physics. Understanding the rules of exponents and the nature of exponential expressions and equations is essential for solving problems in these and many other areas.

## Diving into Polynomials

### Definition and Types of Polynomials
A polynomial is a mathematical expression consisting of variables, coefficients, and the operations of addition, subtraction, and non-negative integer exponentiation of variables. It can be expressed in the form $`a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1x + a_0`$, where $`a_n, a_{n-1}, \ldots, a_1, a_0`$ are constants (coefficients), and $`n`$ is a non-negative integer (the degree of the polynomial).

Types of polynomials are usually defined based on their degree:
- **Constant Polynomial**: Degree 0, e.g., $`7`$.
- **Linear Polynomial**: Degree 1, e.g., $`2x + 3`$.
- **Quadratic Polynomial**: Degree 2, e.g., $`x^2 + 5x + 6`$.
- **Cubic Polynomial**: Degree 3, e.g., $`x^3 - 2x^2 + x - 1`$.
- **Quartic Polynomial**: Degree 4, and so on.

### Polynomial Addition, Subtraction, Multiplication
- **Addition and Subtraction**: These operations are performed by combining like terms (terms with the same power of the variable). For example, adding $`2x^2 + 3x`$ and $`x^2 - 4x + 1`$ results in $`(2x^2 + x^2) + (3x - 4x) + 1 = 3x^2 - x + 1`$.

- **Multiplication**: To multiply polynomials, every term in the first polynomial is multiplied by every term in the second polynomial, followed by combining like terms. For instance, $`(x + 2)(x - 3) = x^2 - 3x + 2x - 6 = x^2 - x - 6`$.

### Polynomial Division and the Remainder Theorem
- **Polynomial Division**: Similar to long division with numbers, polynomial division involves dividing a polynomial by another polynomial. It's used to simplify expressions or to find the remainder when one polynomial is divided by another.

- **The Remainder Theorem**: This theorem states that if a polynomial $`f(x)`$ is divided by a linear divisor of the form $`x - c`$, then the remainder of this division is $`f(c)`$. For example, if dividing $`f(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2`$ by $`x - 1`$, the remainder is $`f(1) = 1^2 - 3(1) + 2 = 0`$.

- **Practical Application of the Remainder Theorem**: The Remainder Theorem is especially useful in evaluating polynomials at a specific point and in factorization of polynomials. For instance, if the remainder is zero, it implies that $`x - c`$ is a factor of the polynomial.

Understanding polynomials is essential in various areas of mathematics, including algebra, calculus, and numerical analysis. The ability to perform operations with polynomials and the application of the Remainder Theorem are foundational skills in these areas. Polynomials are also used in real-world applications like physics (to describe trajectories), economics (to model profit functions), and engineering (to approximate complex functions).

## Factoring Techniques

### Common Factoring Methods
Factoring is the process of breaking down a polynomial into simpler components (factors) that, when multiplied together, give back the original polynomial. Some common factoring methods include:

1. **Greatest Common Factor (GCF)**: Identify the largest factor that is common to all terms in the polynomial. For example, in $`6x^2 + 12x`$, the GCF is $`6x`$. Factoring out $`6x`$ gives $`6x(x + 2)`$.

2. **Grouping**: Used when a polynomial has four or more terms. The terms are grouped in pairs or sets, factored separately, and then combined. For example, to factor $`x^3 + x^2 + 4x + 4`$, group as $`(x^3 + x^2) + (4x + 4)`$, factor out the GCF from each group, and then factor out the common binomial.

3. **Difference of Squares**: This applies to expressions of the form $`a^2 - b^2`$ and factors into $`(a + b)(a - b)`$. For example, $`x^2 - 9`$ factors into $`(x + 3)(x - 3)`$.

4. **Sum/Difference of Cubes**: Used for expressions like $`a^3 + b^3`$ or $`a^3 - b^3`$. The factorization follows specific patterns: $`a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)`$ and $`a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)`$.

### Factorization of Quadratic Polynomials
Quadratic polynomials have the form $`ax^2 + bx + c`$. To factorize them:

1. **Simple Quadratics**: For quadratics like $`x^2 + bx + c`$, find two numbers that multiply to $`c`$ and add up to $`b`$. For example, to factor $`x^2 + 5x + 6`$, find numbers that multiply to 6 and add up to 5, which are 2 and 3. So, it factors into $`(x + 2)(x + 3)`$.

2. **Complex Quadratics**: For $`ax^2 + bx + c`$ where $`a \neq 1`$, use the "AC method". Multiply $`a`$ and $`c`$, find two numbers that multiply to this product and add to $`b`$, and then use grouping to factor.

3. **Completing the Square**: This technique can also be used to transform a quadratic into a perfect square trinomial, making it easier to factor.

### Applications of Factoring in Solving Equations
Factoring is a powerful tool in solving polynomial equations, especially quadratic equations. Once a polynomial is factored, it can be set equal to zero, and the solutions can be found by applying the zero-product property, which states that if a product of factors equals zero, at least one of the factors must be zero. For example, if $`x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0`$ factors into $`(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0`$, the solutions are $`x = 2`$ and $`x = 3`$.

In practical applications, factoring is used in a wide range of fields, from engineering and physics (to solve motion problems or circuit analysis) to economics (to find break-even points). Its utility in breaking down complex expressions into simpler, solvable components makes it a fundamental technique in algebra and higher-level mathematics.

## Complex Numbers Unveiled

### Introduction to Complex Numbers
Complex numbers are an extension of the real numbers and are fundamental in advanced mathematics and engineering. A complex number is of the form $`a + bi`$, where $`a`$ and $`b`$ are real numbers, and $`i`$ is the imaginary unit, defined as the square root of -1 ($`i^2 = -1`$). The real part of the complex number is $`a`$, and the imaginary part is $`bi`$.

### Basic Operations with Complex Numbers
1. **Addition and Subtraction**: These are performed by adding or subtracting the corresponding real and imaginary parts of the complex numbers. For instance, $`(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i`$.

2. **Multiplication**: Multiply complex numbers by expanding the product as in binomial multiplication and using the fact that $`i^2 = -1`$. For example, $`(a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bdi^2 = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i`$.

3. **Division**: To divide one complex number by another, multiply the numerator and the denominator by the conjugate of the denominator. For example, $`\frac{a + bi}{c + di} = \frac{(a + bi)(c - di)}{(c + di)(c - di)} = \frac{(ac + bd) + (bc - ad)i}{c^2 + d^2}`$.

### The Concept of Conjugates and Modulus
1. **Conjugate of a Complex Number**: The conjugate of a complex number $`a + bi`$ is $`a - bi`$. It is obtained by changing the sign of the imaginary part. Conjugates are particularly useful in the division of complex numbers, as they help eliminate the imaginary part from the denominator.

2. **Modulus of a Complex Number**: The modulus (or absolute value) of a complex number $`a + bi`$ is a measure of its size or magnitude, denoted as $`|a + bi|`$ and is defined as $`\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}`$. The modulus represents the distance of the complex number from the origin in the complex plane.

Complex numbers have various applications in physics, engineering, and mathematics. In electrical engineering, they are used to analyze circuits involving alternating current. In physics, they are essential in quantum mechanics and in the description of waves and oscillations. In mathematics, complex numbers provide a more complete understanding of polynomial equations, as every polynomial equation has a root if complex numbers are included. Understanding complex operations, conjugates, and modulus is key to applying complex numbers effectively in these fields.

## Unraveling Quadratic Equations

Quadratic equations are polynomial equations of the second degree, generally in the form $`ax^2 + bx + c = 0`$, where $`a`$, $`b`$, and $`c`$ are constants, and $`a \neq 0`$. There are several methods to solve these equations, each with its unique approach.

### Solving Quadratic Equations by Factoring
Factoring involves expressing the quadratic equation as a product of two binomials. This method works well when the quadratic can be easily factorized. The steps are:

1. Ensure the equation is in standard form $`ax^2 + bx + c = 0`$.
2. Factorize the quadratic expression on the left side.
3. Set each factor equal to zero and solve for $`x`$.

For example, to solve $`x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0`$:
- Factorize to $`(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0`$.
- Set each factor to zero: $`x - 2 = 0`$ and $`x - 3 = 0`$.
- Solve for $`x`$: $`x = 2`$ and $`x = 3`$.

### Completing the Square
Completing the square transforms a quadratic equation into a perfect square trinomial, making it easier to solve. The steps are:

1. Arrange the equation with the quadratic and linear terms on one side: $`ax^2 + bx = -c`$.
2. Divide all terms by $`a`$ (if $`a \neq 1`$) to get the coefficient of $`x^2`$ to be 1.
3. Add $`(\frac{b}{2a})^2`$ to both sides of the equation.
4. Write the left side as a squared binomial.
5. Solve for $`x`$ using square root on both sides.

For example, for $`x^2 - 6x + 8 = 0`$:
- Rearrange to $`x^2 - 6x = -8`$.
- Add $`(\frac{-6}{2})^2 = 9`$ to both sides to get $`x^2 - 6x + 9 = 1`$.
- This simplifies to $`(x - 3)^2 = 1`$.
- Solving $`x - 3 = \pm 1`$ gives $`x = 4`$ or $`x = 2`$.

### Quadratic Formula and Its Derivation
The quadratic formula provides a direct way to solve any quadratic equation. The formula is $`x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}`$. It is derived from the process of completing the square:

1. Start with the standard form: $`ax^2 + bx + c = 0`$.
2. Rearrange: $`ax^2 + bx = -c`$.
3. Divide all terms by $`a`$: $`x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{c}{a}`$.
4. Complete the square by adding $`(\frac{b}{2a})^2`$ to both sides.
5. Rearrange and simplify to get the quadratic formula.

The quadratic formula is particularly useful because it can solve any quadratic equation, including those that are not easily factorizable. It's also crucial in determining the nature of the roots of the equation (real and distinct, real and equal, or complex) based on the discriminant $`b^2 - 4ac`$.

In summary, understanding these three methods—factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula—is essential for effectively solving quadratic equations, which are commonplace in various mathematical, scientific, and engineering contexts.

## Systems of Linear Equations

A system of linear equations consists of two or more linear equations with the same set of variables. The solutions to these systems are the values for the variables that satisfy all the equations simultaneously. There are several methods to solve these systems, each suitable for different types of problems.

### Graphical Method of Solving
The graphical method involves plotting each equation on a coordinate plane and finding the point(s) where the graphs intersect. Each equation is represented by a line, and the intersection points are the solutions to the system. This method is most effective for systems with a small number of variables (usually two) and provides a visual understanding of the solution.

- **Procedure**: Convert each equation into slope-intercept form (y = mx + b), plot them on the graph, and identify the intersection point(s).
- **Limitations**: It's less accurate when the intersection points are not integers or when the lines are nearly parallel (as in the case of systems with no solution or infinitely many solutions).

### Substitution and Elimination Methods
1. **Substitution Method**: This method involves solving one of the equations for one variable and then substituting this expression into the other equation(s). It is particularly useful when one of the equations is already solved for one variable or can be easily manipulated into such a form.

   - **Procedure**: Solve one equation for one variable, substitute this into the other equation, solve for the second variable, and then substitute back to find the first variable.

2. **Elimination Method**: Also known as the addition method, it involves adding or subtracting the equations to eliminate one of the variables. This method is effective when you can easily align terms to cancel out one of the variables.

   - **Procedure**: Manipulate the equations (if necessary) so that adding or subtracting them will eliminate one variable, solve for the remaining variable, and then back-substitute to find the other variable.

### Applications in Real-World Problems
Systems of linear equations are widely used to solve real-world problems across various fields:

1. **Business and Economics**: They are used for cost and revenue analysis, optimization problems, and to solve supply and demand models.

2. **Science and Engineering**: In these fields, systems of linear equations model physical phenomena, electrical circuits, and chemical reactions.

3. **Computer Science**: They are utilized in algorithms, particularly in areas like machine learning, data fitting, and optimization.

4. **Everyday Problem Solving**: These systems can be applied to everyday scenarios like budgeting, planning, and even in tasks like nutrition and diet planning where multiple constraints need to be satisfied simultaneously.

Understanding these methods is crucial as they each have unique advantages depending on the nature of the system being solved. The graphical method offers a visual perspective, the substitution method is efficient for simpler systems, and the elimination method is often the fastest approach for more complex systems.

## Inequalities and Their Solutions

Inequalities are mathematical expressions involving the relations less than (<), greater than (>), less than or equal to (≤), or greater than or equal to (≥). They are used to describe a range of possible values rather than a specific value, as is the case with equations.

### Linear Inequalities
Linear inequalities are similar to linear equations but use inequality signs instead of an equals sign. They generally appear in the form $`ax + b < c`$ or $`ax + b > c`$, where $`a`$, $`b`$, and $`c`$ are constants, and $`x`$ is the variable.

- **Solving Linear Inequalities**: To solve a linear inequality, you manipulate it similarly to a linear equation: isolate the variable on one side of the inequality. However, there's an important rule: when you multiply or divide by a negative number, you must flip the direction of the inequality sign.
- **Solution Representation**: The solution is often represented as an interval or a graph on a number line, indicating a range of values that satisfy the inequality.

### Polynomial and Rational Inequalities
- **Polynomial Inequalities**: These involve polynomial expressions and can look like $`p(x) > 0`$ or $`p(x) < 0`$. The solution involves finding the roots of the polynomial and determining the sign of the polynomial in the intervals defined by these roots.
- **Rational Inequalities**: Inequalities that involve rational expressions, like $`\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} > 0`$. The process of solving them involves finding the zeros and undefined points (where the denominator is zero), and then analyzing the sign of the expression in the intervals defined by these points.

### Graphical Representations
Graphical representation of inequalities provides a visual way to understand the solution set.

- **Linear Inequalities**: Graphing a linear inequality involves graphing the corresponding linear equation (boundary line) and then shading the half-plane that represents the solution. If the inequality is strict (< or >), the boundary line is dashed. If it includes equality (≤ or ≥), the line is solid.
- **Polynomial and Rational Inequalities**: The graph of the polynomial or rational function helps identify where the function is above or below the x-axis, corresponding to positive or negative values. This visual representation assists in understanding the intervals that make the inequality true.

In real-world applications, inequalities are used in various scenarios like defining the permissible ranges for physical quantities, setting thresholds in economics and finance, and establishing constraints in optimization problems. Understanding how to solve and graph inequalities is crucial in many fields, including mathematics, engineering, economics, and the sciences.

## Functions and Their Graphs

### Definition and Types of Functions
A function is a relation between a set of inputs and a set of permissible outputs, where each input is related to exactly one output. In mathematical terms, a function $`f`$ from a set $`X`$ (the domain) to a set $`Y`$ (the codomain) is denoted as $`f: X \to Y`$.

- **Linear Functions**: Represented as $`f(x) = mx + b`$, where $`m`$ and $`b`$ are constants. The graph is a straight line with slope $`m`$ and y-intercept $`b`$.
- **Quadratic Functions**: Have the form $`f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c`$. Their graphs are parabolas, which can open upwards or downwards depending on the sign of $`a`$.
- **Polynomial Functions**: More general than quadratics, these functions have the form $`f(x) = a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1x + a_0`$ and are represented by curves of various shapes.
- **Rational Functions**: Formed by the ratio of two polynomial functions, $`f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}`$, with graphs that can include asymptotes.
- **Exponential Functions**: Expressed as $`f(x) = a^x`$ (where $`a > 0`$) and feature continuous, rapidly increasing or decreasing graphs.
- **Logarithmic Functions**: The inverse of exponential functions, given by $`f(x) = \log_a(x)`$, with graphs that increase slowly and never touch the y-axis.

### Domain and Range of Functions
- **Domain**: The set of all possible input values (x-values) for which the function is defined. It is determined by the nature of the function. For instance, the domain of $`f(x) = \sqrt{x}`$ is $`x \geq 0`$, since the square root of a negative number is not defined in the set of real numbers.
- **Range**: The set of all possible output values (y-values) the function can produce. For example, the range of $`f(x) = x^2`$ is $`y \geq 0`$, as a square of a real number cannot be negative.

### Graphing Basic Functions
Graphing functions involves plotting points that satisfy the function equation and then connecting these points to reveal the overall shape of the graph.

- **Linear Functions**: To graph a linear function, plot the y-intercept and use the slope to find another point. Draw a straight line through these points.
- **Quadratic and Higher Polynomial Functions**: Plot several points by substituting various x-values into the function, then connect these points to form a smooth curve.
- **Rational Functions**: Identify asymptotes and intercepts, plot key points, and sketch the curve, noting how it behaves near asymptotes.
- **Exponential and Logarithmic Functions**: Plot points and observe the rapid increase (exponential) or slow growth (logarithmic), making sure to note asymptotic behavior.

Graphing functions is essential in mathematics, as it provides a visual representation of the behavior of functions. It is widely used in various fields, including physics, engineering, economics, and biology, to model and analyze real-world phenomena. Understanding how to interpret and sketch these graphs is a fundamental skill in studying and applying mathematical concepts.

## Exploring Linear Functions

Linear functions are fundamental in mathematics and are used to model relationships where a constant rate of change is observed. They are typically represented in the form $`y = mx + b`$, where $`m`$ is the slope and $`b`$ is the y-intercept.

### Slope and Intercepts
1. **Slope (m)**: The slope of a linear function represents the rate of change of the function, indicating how much $`y`$ changes for a unit change in $`x`$. It is calculated as the ratio of the change in $`y`$ (rise) to the change in $`x`$ (run), often expressed as $`m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}`$. A positive slope indicates an increasing function, while a negative slope indicates a decreasing function.

2. **Y-Intercept (b)**: The y-intercept is the point where the line crosses the y-axis. It represents the value of $`y`$ when $`x = 0`$.

3. **X-Intercept**: The point where the line crosses the x-axis, representing the value of $`x`$ when $`y = 0`$. It can be found by setting $`y = 0`$ in the equation and solving for $`x`$.

### Graphing Linear Functions
To graph a linear function:
1. Plot the y-intercept ($`b`$) on the y-axis.
2. Use the slope ($`m`$) to determine another point on the line. From the y-intercept, move up or down (depending on the sign of $`m`$) and right or left to find a second point.
3. Draw a straight line through these points, extending it across the graph.

### Applications in Real Life
Linear functions have numerous practical applications, including:

1. **Finance**: Calculating interest, budgeting, and cost analysis. For example, determining total cost as a linear function of the number of items purchased.

2. **Science**: In physics, linear functions describe constant-speed motion; in chemistry, they model direct proportionality in reactions.

3. **Economics**: Supply and demand models, cost functions, and revenue calculations often use linear functions.

4. **Statistics and Data Analysis**: Linear regression, a method for modeling the relationship between two variables, is based on linear functions.

5. **Everyday Calculations**: Calculating distances for travel, converting currencies, or scaling recipes can involve linear functions.

Understanding linear functions and their graphical representation is crucial for interpreting and solving problems in these areas. The concept of slope and intercepts provides a clear understanding of how variables are related and how changes in one variable affect the other.

## Delving into Quadratic Functions

Quadratic functions represent a fundamental concept in algebra and are widely used in various scientific and mathematical applications. They are typically written in the form $`y = ax^2 + bx + c`$, where $`a`$, $`b`$, and $`c`$ are constants, and $`a \neq 0`$.

### Properties of Quadratic Functions
1. **Parabolic Shape**: The graph of a quadratic function is a parabola. If $`a > 0`$, the parabola opens upwards, and if $`a < 0`$, it opens downwards.

2. **Vertex**: The vertex is the highest or lowest point on the parabola, depending on whether the parabola opens downwards or upwards, respectively. The vertex can be found using the formula $`x = -\frac{b}{2a}`$ for the x-coordinate, and then substituting this back into the function to find the y-coordinate.

3. **Axis of Symmetry**: This is a vertical line that passes through the vertex and divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves. Its equation is $`x = -\frac{b}{2a}`$.

4. **Y-Intercept**: The y-intercept is the point where the graph intersects the y-axis, given by the value of $`c`$ in the quadratic equation.

5. **Roots/Zeros/X-Intercepts**: These are the points where the parabola intersects the x-axis. They can be found by solving the equation $`ax^2 + bx + c = 0`$ using various methods like factoring, completing the square, or using the quadratic formula.

### Graphing Parabolas
To graph a quadratic function:
1. Identify the coefficients $`a`$, $`b`$, and $`c`$ in the quadratic equation.
2. Calculate the vertex and the axis of symmetry.
3. Plot the vertex and a few points on either side of the vertex. Since the parabola is symmetrical, points will mirror across the axis of symmetry.
4. Determine the y-intercept ($`c`$) and, if possible, the x-intercepts by solving the quadratic equation.
5. Connect these points with a smooth curve to form the parabola.

### Real-world Applications
Quadratic functions are used in various practical situations, including:
1. **Physics**: Modeling the motion of objects under gravity, such as the path of a ball thrown in the air, which follows a parabolic trajectory.
2. **Engineering**: In designing structures and engineering components, such as parabolic arches and bridges, where stress distributions can be modeled using quadratic functions.
3. **Economics and Business**: Quadratic functions model various economic behaviors, including cost, revenue, and profit calculations when these are not linear.
4. **Astronomy and Optics**: Describing the shapes of satellite dishes and lenses, which often involve parabolic shapes to focus signals and light.
5. **Ballistics**: Predicting the trajectory of projectiles, where air resistance is negligible, resulting in a parabolic path.

Understanding quadratic functions and their properties, such as the shape and position of their graphs, is essential in interpreting and solving problems in these and many other fields. The ability to graph these functions provides a valuable tool for visualizing and analyzing the relationships they describe.

## Polynomials and Rational Functions

### Polynomials
Polynomial functions are expressions consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only the operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. They can be expressed in the form $`f(x) = a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1x + a_0`$.

##### Graphing Polynomial Functions
1. **Identify Characteristics**: Note the degree of the polynomial (highest power of $`x`$) and leading coefficient (coefficient of the term with the highest power). These determine the end behavior of the graph.
2. **Find Intercepts**: Calculate the y-intercept by evaluating $`f(0)`$ and x-intercepts (roots) by solving $`f(x) = 0`$.
3. **Determine Symmetry**: Check for symmetry. If the function is an even degree and all the powers of $`x`$ are even, it’s symmetric about the y-axis. If it’s odd and all powers are odd, it’s symmetric about the origin.
4. **Plot Critical Points**: Identify and plot any maxima, minima, and inflection points by analyzing the first and second derivatives, if possible.
5. **Sketch the Graph**: Plot enough points to understand the shape of the graph and draw a smooth curve through these points, considering the end behavior and critical points.

### Rational Functions
Rational functions are ratios of polynomial functions, of the form $`f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}`$ where $`p(x)`$ and $`q(x)`$ are polynomial functions, and $`q(x) \neq 0`$.

##### Behavior of Rational Functions
1. **Vertical Asymptotes**: Occur at values of $`x`$ where $`q(x) = 0`$, as long as those values do not also make $`p(x) = 0`$. The function approaches infinity or negative infinity near these points.
2. **Horizontal Asymptotes**: Determined by the degrees of $`p(x)`$ and $`q(x)`$. If the degree of $`p(x)`$ is less than that of $`q(x)`$, the x-axis (y=0) is a horizontal asymptote. If they have equal degrees, the horizontal asymptote is $`y = \frac{\text{leading coefficient of } p(x)}{\text{leading coefficient of } q(x)}`$.
3. **Holes**: Occur where both $`p(x)`$ and $`q(x)`$ are zero, provided the common factor can be canceled out.
4. **Intercepts**: Find the y-intercept by evaluating $`f(0)`$ and x-intercepts by setting the numerator equal to zero and solving for $`x`$.

### Long-run Behavior of Polynomials
The long-run behavior of polynomial functions is determined primarily by the degree and leading coefficient:
1. **Even Degree**: If the leading coefficient is positive, the ends of the graph rise to infinity. If it’s negative, they fall to negative infinity.
2. **Odd Degree**: If the leading coefficient is positive, the left end of the graph falls to negative infinity, and the right end rises to positive infinity. If it's negative, the left end rises to positive infinity, and the right end falls to negative infinity.

In conclusion, both polynomial and rational functions have distinct characteristics that affect their graphs. Understanding these properties, such as end behavior, asymptotes, and intercepts, is crucial for accurately sketching their graphs and interpreting their behavior, which is essential in various applications in science, engineering, economics, and other fields.

## Exponential and Logarithmic Functions

### Understanding Exponential Growth and Decay
Exponential functions are mathematical expressions of the form $`f(x) = a \cdot b^x`$, where $`a`$ is a constant, $`b`$ is the base of the exponential (a positive real number), and $`x`$ is the exponent.

1. **Exponential Growth**: Occurs when the base $`b > 1`$. In this scenario, the value of the function grows rapidly as $`x`$ increases. This is seen in phenomena like population growth, compound interest, and certain types of technological advancements.

2. **Exponential Decay**: Occurs when $`0 < b < 1`$. Here, the value of the function decreases rapidly as $`x`$ increases. Exponential decay models are common in radioactive decay, depreciation of assets, and cooling processes.

### Introduction to Logarithms
A logarithm is the inverse operation to exponentiation. The logarithm of a number is the exponent to which another fixed number, the base, must be raised to produce that number. For a base $`b`$ and a number $`y`$, the logarithm is written as $`\log_b(y)`$ and satisfies the equation $`b^{\log_b(y)} = y`$.

- **Common Bases**: The most common bases for logarithms are base $`e`$ (the natural logarithm, denoted as $`\ln(y)`$) and base 10 (the common logarithm, denoted as $`\log(y)`$).

### Properties and Graphs of Exponential and Logarithmic Functions
1. **Exponential Functions**:
   - The graph of $`f(x) = a \cdot b^x`$ is a smooth curve.
   - It passes through the point (0, $`a`$) since $`b^0 = 1`$.
   - The function never touches the x-axis (asymptotic to the x-axis), meaning it has no x-intercepts.
   - The rate of increase (or decrease) is proportional to the current value, leading to a rapid change.

2. **Logarithmic Functions**:
   - The graph of $`f(x) = \log_b(x)`$ is also a smooth curve.
   - It passes through the point (1, 0) since $`\log_b(1) = 0`$.
   - The function has a vertical asymptote at x=0, meaning it is undefined for $`x \leq 0`$.
   - The curve approaches the y-axis but never crosses it.

Both exponential and logarithmic functions are extensively used in various scientific fields, including biology (to model population dynamics), chemistry (to understand reaction rates), physics (to describe phenomena with exponential growth or decay), and in finance (to calculate compound interest and growth rates). Their graphs provide a visual representation of their behavior, which is pivotal in understanding the underlying processes they model.

## Radical Functions and Equations

### Definition and Properties of Radicals
Radical functions involve roots, such as square roots, cube roots, etc. The most common radical function is the square root function, denoted as $`f(x) = \sqrt{x}`$.

1. **Roots**: The $`n`$-th root of a number $`a`$ is written as $`\sqrt[n]{a}`$ and is the number that, when raised to the power $`n`$, gives $`a`$. For example, $`\sqrt[3]{8} = 2`$ because $`2^3 = 8`$.
2. **Properties of Square Roots**:
   - For $`a \geq 0`$, $`\sqrt{a}`$ is always non-negative.
   - $`\sqrt{a \cdot b} = \sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b}`$ for $`a, b \geq 0`$.
   - $`\sqrt{\frac{a}{b}} = \frac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}}`$ for $`a \geq 0`$ and $`b > 0`$.
3. **Properties of Higher-Order Roots**: For odd roots, such as cube roots, the radicand (the number under the root) can be negative. For example, $`\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2`$.

### Solving Radical Equations
Radical equations are equations in which the variable is under a radical. To solve them:

1. **Isolate the Radical**: If possible, rearrange the equation so that the radical expression is by itself on one side of the equation.
2. **Eliminate the Radical**: Raise both sides of the equation to the power of the root to eliminate the radical. For a square root, square both sides; for a cube root, cube both sides, and so on.
3. **Solve the Resulting Equation**: Once the radical is eliminated, you'll have a polynomial or another type of equation to solve.
4. **Check for Extraneous Solutions**: Always substitute your answers back into the original equation to check for extraneous solutions, as squaring both sides can introduce invalid solutions.

### Graphing Radical Functions
The graph of a radical function, particularly a square root function, has some distinct characteristics:

1. **Square Root Function ($`f(x) = \sqrt{x}`$)**: 
   - The graph starts at the origin (0,0) and increases slowly to the right.
   - It is defined only for $`x \geq 0`$ and $`y \geq 0`$ since you can't take the square root of a negative number in the real number system.
   - The curve is concave down and approaches, but never touches, the x-axis and y-axis.

2. **Higher-Order Roots**: 
   - For even-order roots (like $`\sqrt[4]{x}`$), the graph is similar to the square root function but grows more slowly.
   - For odd-order roots (like $`\sqrt[3]{x}`$), the graph extends to the left of the y-axis, reflecting that odd roots are defined for negative numbers.

Radical functions are used in various fields of science and engineering, especially in physics and geometry, where they often arise in formulas and equations involving distances and areas. Their graphs help in visualizing the behavior of these functions, particularly in understanding how they grow and in what domain they are defined.

## Sequences and Series

### Sequences and Series

### Arithmetic and Geometric Sequences
1. **Arithmetic Sequences**: An arithmetic sequence is a sequence of numbers in which the difference between consecutive terms is constant. This constant difference is called the common difference, denoted as $`d`$. The $`n`$-th term of an arithmetic sequence can be found using the formula $`a_n = a_1 + (n - 1)d`$, where $`a_1`$ is the first term.

2. **Geometric Sequences**: A geometric sequence is a sequence where each term after the first is found by multiplying the previous term by a fixed, non-zero number called the common ratio, denoted as $`r`$. The $`n`$-th term of a geometric sequence is given by $`a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{n-1}`$, where $`a_1`$ is the first term.

### Summation of Series
1. **Arithmetic Series**: The sum of the first $`n`$ terms of an arithmetic sequence (arithmetic series) is given by $`S_n = \frac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n)`$ or $`S_n = \frac{n}{2}[2a_1 + (n-1)d]`$, where $`S_n`$ is the sum of the first $`n`$ terms.

2. **Geometric Series**: The sum of the first $`n`$ terms of a geometric sequence (geometric series) is given by $`S_n = a_1 \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r}`$ for $`r \neq 1`$. If $`r = 1`$, the sum is simply $`n \cdot a_1`$.

### Applications in Finance and Sciences
1. **Finance**:
   - **Arithmetic Sequences and Series**: Used in calculating amortization schedules, where payments are made at a constant interval.
   - **Geometric Sequences and Series**: Essential in calculating compound interest, where the value of an investment grows exponentially over time.

2. **Sciences**:
   - **Arithmetic Sequences**: Can be used in fields like biology or chemistry, for example, in measuring substances that degrade by a constant amount over equal time intervals.
   - **Geometric Sequences**: Common in modeling population growth in biology, radioactive decay in physics, or the concentration of a reactant in a chemical reaction over time.

Sequences and series are fundamental concepts in mathematics with extensive applications in various practical fields. Understanding their nature and how to sum them provides a powerful tool for analysis and prediction in finance, science, and beyond. The concepts of arithmetic and geometric progressions are particularly important in scenarios that involve regular growth or decay.

## An Introduction to Probability

Probability is a branch of mathematics that deals with calculating the likelihood of a given event's occurrence, which is expressed as a number between 0 and 1. 

### Basic Probability Concepts
1. **Experiment**: An action or procedure that produces well-defined outcomes.
2. **Sample Space (S)**: The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
3. **Event**: A subset of the sample space. It can include one or more outcomes.
4. **Probability of an Event (P)**: Defined as $`P(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in E}}{\text{total number of outcomes in S}}`$, where $`E`$ is an event in sample space $`S`$. It ranges from 0 (impossibility) to 1 (certainty).

### Combinations and Permutations
1. **Permutations**: A permutation is an arrangement of objects in a specific order. The formula for the number of permutations of $`n`$ objects taken $`r`$ at a time is $`P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}`$, where $`n!`$ (n factorial) is the product of all positive integers up to $`n`$.

2. **Combinations**: A combination is a selection of objects without regard to the order in which they are selected. The formula for the number of combinations of $`n`$ objects taken $`r`$ at a time is $`C(n, r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}`$.

### Applications in Statistics
Probability forms the theoretical foundation of statistics, which is the practice of analyzing data to infer properties about the population:

1. **Data Analysis**: Probability is used to infer trends and make predictions based on data.
2. **Hypothesis Testing**: Used to determine the probability that a given hypothesis is true based on sample data.
3. **Decision Making**: In business and economics, probability models are used to make informed decisions under uncertainty.
4. **Quality Control**: In manufacturing, probability helps in assessing the quality of products and the reliability of processes.

Probability is also extensively used in fields such as finance (risk assessment and modeling), computer science (algorithms and cryptography), and even everyday decision making (evaluating risks and chances in various situations). Understanding basic probability concepts, along with permutations and combinations, is crucial for interpreting data and making predictions based on it.

## The World of Algebraic Structures

### Introduction to Matrices
A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers, symbols, or expressions, arranged in rows and columns. Matrices are fundamental in various fields of algebra, and they are particularly useful in representing and solving systems of linear equations, among other applications.

- **Components**: A matrix is typically denoted by a capital letter and its elements are usually denoted by lowercase letters with two subscripts. For example, in matrix $`A`$, the element in the $`i`$-th row and $`j`$-th column is $`a_{ij}`$.
- **Size**: The size of a matrix is given in terms of its rows and columns, typically denoted as $`m \times n`$, where $`m`$ is the number of rows and $`n`$ is the number of columns.

### Basic Operations with Matrices
1. **Addition and Subtraction**: Matrices can be added or subtracted if they have the same dimensions. The operations are performed element-wise.

2. **Scalar Multiplication**: A matrix can be multiplied by a scalar (a single number). Each element of the matrix is multiplied by that scalar.

3. **Matrix Multiplication**: The product of two matrices is defined if the number of columns in the first matrix equals the number of rows in the second matrix. The resulting matrix has dimensions based on the rows of the first matrix and the columns of the second.

4. **Transpose**: The transpose of a matrix $`A`$, denoted as $`A^T`$, is formed by turning rows into columns and columns into rows.

### Determinants and Inverses
1. **Determinants**: The determinant is a scalar value that is a function of the entries of a square matrix. It provides important information about the matrix, such as whether the matrix is invertible and the volume change factor under the linear transformation the matrix represents. The determinant of a 2x2 matrix $`A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}`$ is calculated as $`ad - bc`$.

2. **Inverses**: The inverse of a square matrix $`A`$, denoted as $`A^{-1}`$, is a matrix such that $`AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I`$, where $`I`$ is the identity matrix. A matrix has an inverse if and only if its determinant is non-zero. The inverse is used to solve matrix equations and in many areas of applied mathematics.

Matrices, their operations, determinants, and inverses are widely used in various areas, including physics, computer graphics, statistics, economics, and engineering. Understanding these concepts is crucial for solving linear systems, performing linear transformations, and analyzing data.

## Glossary of Terms

**Variable**: A symbol, typically a letter, used to represent an unknown or arbitrary number.

**Constant**: A fixed value that does not change.

**Coefficient**: A number used to multiply a variable.

**Expression**: A combination of variables, constants, and operations without an equality sign.

**Equation**: A mathematical statement that asserts the equality of two expressions.

**Function**: A relation between a set of inputs and a set of permissible outputs, with each input related to exactly one output.

**Linear Equation**: An equation of the first degree, meaning it contains only linear terms (no variable is raised to a power greater than one).

**Quadratic Equation**: An equation where the highest exponent of the variable is two.

**Polynomial**: An expression consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only the operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents of variables.

**Rational Expression**: A ratio of two polynomial expressions.

**Inequality**: A mathematical statement that compares two expressions with inequality signs like <, >, ≤, or ≥.

**Matrix**: A rectangular array of numbers, symbols, or expressions arranged in rows and columns.

**Determinant**: A special number calculated from a square matrix.

**Inverse**: In the context of matrices, the matrix that, when multiplied with the original matrix, results in the identity matrix.

**Domain**: The set of all possible input values for a function.

**Range**: The set of all possible output values of a function.

**Slope**: A measure of the steepness of a line, typically represented as the ratio of the rise over the run.

**Root or Zero**: The value of the variable that makes a polynomial equal to zero.

**Factorization**: The process of breaking down a polynomial into simpler polynomials that, when multiplied together, give the original polynomial.

**Series**: The sum of the terms of a sequence.
